Chapter 01

There are 31 satellites above your head right now

At this moment, roughly 20,200 km above you, there are 31 operational GPS satellites orbiting Earth. They circle the planet twice every day, travelling at about 14,000 km/h. The US Space Force operates them and they've been running since the 1970s. Anyone with a receiver can use them for free.

The satellites are arranged in 6 orbital planes tilted at 55° from the equator. This arrangement ensures that from almost anywhere on Earth's surface, you can always see at least 4 satellites in the sky — usually 8 to 12. Seeing the sky matters because GPS signals can't pass through solid objects well.

Chapter 02

GPS is not a map — it's a clock system

This is the most important thing to understand. Satellites don't know where you are and they don't send you your location. What they send is time. Extremely precise time.

Each satellite carries 4 atomic clocks accurate to about 1 nanosecond (one billionth of a second). Continuously, every satellite broadcasts a message that essentially says: "I am satellite #5. Right now, according to my atomic clock, the time is exactly 14:22:33.000000000."

Your phone receives that message and looks at its own clock. The signal traveled at the speed of light — 299,792,458 metres per second. So if the message took 0.067 seconds to arrive, the satellite must be 0.067 × 299,792,458 = 20,086,195 metres away from you.

Core Principle

Distance = Speed × Time. The speed of light is constant and known. Your phone measures the travel time. Distance follows automatically. The satellite's position in space is calculated from its orbital data. Therefore your position can be found.

Analogy

Imagine you hear a thunderclap. Sound travels at 343 m/s. If you count 3 seconds from the lightning flash to the thunder, the storm is 3 × 343 = 1,029 metres away. GPS is exactly this — using light instead of sound, and satellites instead of lightning. The precision just needs to be a billion times better.

Chapter 03

One distance isn't enough — you need four satellites

Knowing you're 20,000 km from satellite #5 tells you that you're somewhere on a sphere of radius 20,000 km centred on that satellite. That's a huge area — the whole shell of a sphere the size of a planet.

Add a second satellite at a different position. You're also 22,000 km from satellite #12. Now you're on the intersection of two spheres — a circle in space. Still millions of possible locations.

Add a third satellite. The intersection of three spheres gives just two points. One of them is deep in space, far from Earth. The phone discards it. You're at the remaining point on or near Earth's surface.

The fourth satellite is needed to correct for your phone's imperfect clock. Your phone's clock is not an atomic clock — it drifts. The fourth satellite's equation lets the phone solve for its own clock error simultaneously with its position. Without 4 satellites, the solution is wrong.

Exactly right. The receiver solves 4 unknowns simultaneously: X, Y, Z position plus its own clock offset Δt. You need 4 equations (4 satellites) to solve for 4 unknowns. This is why your phone can have a cheap quartz clock and still get accurate GPS positions.
Chapter 04

The signal is a radio wave at 1575.42 MHz

The satellite sends its time signal as a radio wave. Specifically, it transmits on a carrier frequency of 1,575,420,000 Hz — the wave oscillates 1.575 billion times per second. This is the GPS "L1" band. It sits between FM radio (~100 MHz) and microwave ovens (2,450 MHz).

A radio wave is just an oscillating electromagnetic field moving through space at the speed of light. The frequency determines how fast it oscillates; the wavelength is how long one complete cycle is. At 1575.42 MHz, the wavelength is about 19 cm — roughly the length of your hand.

RADIO WAVE — CARRIER AT 1575.42 MHz (visualised, not to scale)
The raw carrier carries no information — it's just a blank radio wave at 1575.42 MHz.

A blank carrier wave carries no information. To put the timing data onto the wave, GPS uses a technique called Binary Phase Shift Keying (BPSK). When the data bit is a 1, the wave continues normally. When the data bit is a 0, the wave's phase instantly flips by 180° — it's like pressing a "invert" button on the wave. Your receiver detects these phase flips and reads the data.

But GPS doesn't just BPSK the raw data bits directly. First it applies a spreading code at a much higher rate. This is the clever part — and it's covered in chapter 6.

Chapter 05

31 satellites, one frequency — spread spectrum keeps them separate

Here's a puzzle: all 31 GPS satellites broadcast on 1575.42 MHz simultaneously. How does your phone tell them apart? If your neighbours all talk at the same volume in the same room, you can't hear any individual conversation.

The answer is Code Division Multiple Access (CDMA). Each satellite is given a unique mathematical code — a specific pattern of 1,023 ones and zeros that repeats every millisecond. Before transmitting, every data bit is multiplied by this unique code at 1,023,000 times per second. The signal is "spread" across a wider bandwidth.

Analogy

Imagine 31 people in a room all talking at once — total chaos. But now give each person a unique "accent" — a specific rhythmic pattern they weave into everything they say. Now you can tune your ear to one specific accent and understand only that person, filtering out all others. The C/A code is GPS's version of a unique accent.

CDMA — MULTIPLE SATELLITES ON THE SAME FREQUENCY
Correlating with:
Only PRN 1 correlates — the others cancel out. This is how your phone isolates one satellite from 31 transmitting simultaneously.

When the phone wants to find satellite #5, it generates PRN 5's known code pattern locally. It multiplies the received signal by this local copy and integrates over 1 millisecond. If PRN 5 is present, the result is a large peak. If it's any other satellite, the cross-correlation is nearly zero — they cancel. This is the remarkable property of Gold codes, designed by Robert Gold in 1967 specifically for this purpose.

Chapter 06

The C/A code — each satellite's unique fingerprint

The C/A code (Coarse/Acquisition) for each satellite is generated by two 10-stage shift registers called G1 and G2. A shift register is just a row of 10 memory cells (bits) that shift right every clock tick, with the new leftmost bit computed from XOR-ing specific cells.

Both registers start as all-ones and run at 1,023,000 clocks per second. After exactly 1,023 clocks, they return to their starting state and repeat. That's why the code length is 1,023 chips — it completes one full cycle in exactly 1 millisecond.

C/A CODE GENERATION — STEP THROUGH IT
Chip 0/1023
G1 and G2 shift right each clock. The output chip is G1[stage10] XOR G2[tap_i XOR tap_j]. Each PRN has unique G2 taps from IS-GPS-200N Table 3-Ia.

What makes each satellite unique is which two cells of G2 are XOR-ed together before combining with G1's output. For PRN 1 it's cells 2 and 6. For PRN 5 it's cells 1 and 9. These tap assignments are published in IS-GPS-200N Table 3-Ia and have been fixed since the 1970s. Every GPS receiver on Earth knows them.

Chapter 07

The navigation message — what the satellite actually says

The C/A code is just a carrier wave spread across a wider bandwidth — it's the "accent" that identifies the satellite. Inside it, hidden at 50 bits per second, is the actual message: the navigation message.

50 bits per second is incredibly slow. A single text message would take about 30 seconds to transmit. But the data is small — it's just numbers describing the satellite's orbit and clock. These numbers are called ephemeris data.

What the Navigation Message Contains

Subframe 1 — The satellite's clock error corrections. Even atomic clocks drift slightly; these correction terms let your phone adjust for it.

Subframes 2 & 3 — The satellite's orbital parameters: the shape and orientation of its orbit, where it is on that orbit right now, and how fast it's moving. Your phone uses these to compute the satellite's ECEF position in space.

Subframes 4 & 5 — Almanac data (rough positions of all other satellites) and ionospheric correction models.

One complete message frame is 1,500 bits taking 30 seconds to transmit. Your phone needs to receive this before it knows where the satellite is in space — before it can use that satellite's range measurement. This is why GPS cold-start takes 30–60 seconds: it's waiting to receive the navigation message.

NAVIGATION MESSAGE LAYER — HOW BITS NEST INSIDE THE C/A CODE
Each navigation bit lasts 20 milliseconds = 20 full repetitions of the 1023-chip C/A code. The receiver averages these to reliably read the bit even through noise.
Chapter 08

The complete signal — three layers multiplied together

Now we can describe the full GPS signal. It's three things multiplied together:

1

The carrier wave — a pure 1575.42 MHz radio wave. On its own it carries no information; it's the medium everything else rides on.

2

The C/A code — the satellite's unique 1,023-chip fingerprint, repeating at 1.023 Mcps. This is XOR-multiplied onto the carrier using BPSK, spreading the signal across a wider bandwidth and making it look like noise to anyone who doesn't have the matching code.

3

The navigation data — 50 bps of orbital and clock data, again BPSK-modulated. Each bit flips or doesn't flip the phase of the spreading code.

SIGNAL LAYERS — CLICK EACH LAYER TO SEE ITS EFFECT
The carrier alone is just a sine wave at 1575.42 MHz — no information.

The received signal power at Earth's surface is around −130 dBm — that's 0.0000000000001 watts. It's so weak that the raw GPS signal looks like noise in a spectrum analyser. Only by knowing the exact C/A code sequence and correlating against it does the signal emerge from the noise floor. This is actually a feature: GPS signals are nearly impossible to jam without generating massive broadband noise.

Chapter 09

What the bladeRF spoofer actually does

Now you understand GPS, you can understand exactly what this project does. The bladeRF is an SDR — a Software Defined Radio — with a transmitter that can send any signal we generate in software at any frequency up to 6 GHz.

Our software generates a completely fake GPS environment. We pick a target location — say, Salt Lake City, Utah. Then, for each GPS satellite currently in the sky, we compute what its signal would look like if you were standing in Utah:

1

Download real orbital data (RINEX file from IGS). This tells us where every satellite actually is in space right now, using Keplerian orbital mechanics.

2

Compute satellite-to-Utah distances. Using the satellite ECEF positions and Utah's ECEF coordinates, we compute the geometric range ρ for each satellite.

3

Set code phase offsets. τ = ρ/c × 1,023,000 chips. Each satellite's C/A code starts at this chip offset, encoding the fake distance.

4

Set Doppler shifts. Even though our fake receiver isn't moving, the real satellites are. We compute the Doppler shift fD for each satellite based on its velocity relative to Utah.

5

Generate fake navigation messages. The ephemeris data must match the real satellites — we embed this from the RINEX file. Clock corrections and parity must be computed correctly or the phone rejects the message.

6

Synthesise I/Q samples. All 8–10 visible satellite channels are computed simultaneously and summed into complex baseband samples at 2.048 Msps.

7

Stream to bladeRF. The bladeRF's DAC converts the digital samples to an analogue signal, the internal mixer upconverts it to 1575.42 MHz, and the antenna transmits.

8

Phone receives, correlates, solves. The phone's GPS chip finds all visible PRN codes, measures the code phase offsets, solves the system of equations — and reports that it's standing in Salt Lake City, Utah.

Why it works

The phone has no way to verify where signals come from — it can only check if the signal is internally consistent (correct parity, correct Doppler, consistent geometry across all satellites). A well-built spoofer satisfies all these checks. The phone believes it because every signal it would check passes. This is why this project must only be operated inside an RF-shielded enclosure — any leak would affect real receivers outside.
